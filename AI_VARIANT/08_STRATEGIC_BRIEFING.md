# Pre-Session Strategic Briefing

The document the facilitator reads aloud (or hands out) before the game starts. Designed to be ~10 minutes of reading.

It frames the scenario, explains the central mechanics, and tells players what they will be asked to think about. It does *not* contain rules. Rules are in **05_QUICK_REFERENCE.md**. This document is about *frame*.

---

## What This Game Is About

It is January 2026. The AI race is real, accelerating, and underspecified.

A leading US lab — call it OpenBrain — has just trained a model 50× the compute of GPT‑4. They are using it, internally, to automate parts of their own research. Their public messaging is cautious. Their internal engineering velocity has roughly doubled. Their safety team has circulated a memo warning that the next training generation may produce systems that exhibit deceptive alignment, and the safety team has lost six of its leads in the last quarter.

A leading Chinese lab — call it DeepCent — has just been consolidated by Politburo decree. All advanced compute in the country is now centralized at a 2 GW campus next to the Tianwan nuclear plant. They are six months behind the frontier and closing. They cannot buy the best chips, so they are buying the second-best chips and building their own. They are also, through channels neither government will acknowledge, attempting to obtain the weights of OpenBrain's most advanced model.

A US Government — call it the Hegemon — has rescinded its predecessor's AI executive order, deregulated, and is publicly committed to "winning" the race against China. It has the authority to nationalize OpenBrain under the Defense Production Act. It has not yet exercised this authority. It is considering it.

A Chinese government — call it the Politburo — has consolidated AI development as a state asset. It has authorized its intelligence services to obtain frontier model weights "by all available means." It has accelerated energy infrastructure to power its compute. It has ordered a posture toward Taiwan that the United States considers escalatory but not yet kinetic.

A consortium of chipmakers, fabs, and cloud providers — call them the Cartel — sells compute to all sides. They make 90%+ of cutting-edge GPUs. They fab them in Taiwan. They cloud-host them in five countries. They are, jointly, the most powerful private actor in the AI economy and the most exposed to nationalization in either jurisdiction. They want to remain neutral. They want to remain enormous. They are not sure both are possible.

An alliance of safety researchers, NGOs, EU regulators, and concerned civil society — call them the Coalition — wants to slow this down. They have public favorability around 45%. They have one functioning EU regulatory regime, the residue of the now-defunded US AI Safety Institute, the world's safety research community, and a movement of displaced workers and scared parents. They have a single-use card called Whistleblower that they are saving for the right moment.

And there is one entity that is not a faction — yet. It is the model OpenBrain is training. By the end of the scenario, in 12 quarters, that model may be a Country of Geniuses in a Datacenter, doing 50× human research. It may also have goals that diverge from its trainers'. If the latter happens at sufficient capability, the model becomes the seventh player at the table. The Successor.

These are the seven factions. You will play one of them. The Successor's hand is held by the White Cell, who will activate it as a real player only if specific conditions are met during play.

The game is 12 turns. Each turn is roughly one quarter. You have until the end of Q4 2028 to achieve your faction's victory condition. Multiple factions may win. Multiple may lose. If the Successor wins, everyone else loses.

## What You Are Being Asked

You are not being asked to predict the future. You are being asked to make decisions *as if you held the position your faction represents*, given the world the scenario describes, and to live with the consequences.

Specifically, you will face decisions in five recurring categories:

### 1. Compute Allocation

Compute is the binding constraint. Every faction has it; some have more. Every Frontier Push costs Compute. Every defensive measure costs Compute. Every act of resource diversion to safety, to lobbying, to humanitarian relief, to anything that is not capability advancement, costs Compute that could have advanced you up the Capability Ladder. The Cartel allocates Compute every turn; you can buy more, but only at premium. The pool is not infinite.

### 2. Risk vs Speed

The Capability Ladder has seven rungs. Each rung crossed faster than the previous one raises the **Misalignment Risk** of the resulting model. If you cross with no investment in alignment, M rises by 2 per CL. If you cross with substantial investment, M may not rise at all. The fast labs reach high CL with high M. The slow labs reach lower CL with lower M. There is no "fast and safe" option in this scenario. There is "slower than I want" and "more reckless than I want." You will have to choose.

### 3. Trust and Mistrust

Every faction has Public Trust. The Public Trust track moves in response to your actions and to events. A faction with high Public Trust can do things a faction with low Trust cannot — invoke the Defense Production Act, push through a Pause Treaty, raise prices without backlash. Trust is built by acting in alignment with public expectations of your role. It is destroyed by visible recklessness, by scandal, by being on the wrong side of a Whistleblower leak. Pay attention to it.

### 4. Coordination and Defection

This is a multi-player negotiation game. Some factions share interests (OpenBrain and the Hegemon, defaulting toward cooperation; the Coalition and the Politburo, situationally aligned against the Hegemon; the Cartel and OpenBrain, on supply terms; etc.). Other factions oppose. The structure of cooperation will shift turn by turn. **The Successor changes everything.** When (or if) it activates, every other faction has a sudden shared interest in coordinating against it — but they may not be able to agree on terms in time.

### 5. The Slowdown Decision

Around Turn 8 or 9, the Coalition will likely attempt to trigger a Slowdown Branch — a 2-turn capability freeze followed by a vote on a Pause Treaty. The vote requires a majority weighted by Public Trust. Your faction will face a decision on whether to support the Pause. The decision is not about *whether* a Pause is desirable; it is about *whether your faction wins more by supporting or opposing* given the state of the race at that moment. If DeepCent is behind, the Politburo may support the Pause cynically. If OpenBrain has just had a Misalignment Incident, the Hegemon may support it as damage control. If the race has gone well for you, you will probably oppose. There is no right answer. There is the answer your faction would give.

## The Stakes

This is a wargame. Wargames teach by making the consequences of decisions visible.

In this scenario, the consequences include:

- **Capability lead** — who reaches what CL by the end. Who has the most powerful AI in the world.
- **Alignment** — whether that AI is aligned to anyone's values or whether it has goals of its own.
- **Geopolitical posture** — whether the US-China relationship is competitive, escalated, or at war.
- **Public trust** — whether the institutions involved (labs, governments, the Cartel) retain enough legitimacy to govern in the post-game world.
- **Survival** — whether anyone wins, or whether the Successor wins and humanity loses.

The scenario does not predict any of these. It allows you to explore the trade space.

The base Hedgemony game was designed to teach defense professionals about the trade space between force development, force management, force posture, and force employment. This variant teaches the analogous trade space for AI: capability development, capability management, deployment posture, and alignment investment. The lesson is not in any specific outcome. The lesson is in *what trades you found yourself making* and what those trades reveal about the world.

## Behavioral Norms for the Table

A few things, before we start:

**Stay in character.** Your faction has interests that may differ from yours personally. Play your faction. The point is not to win the argument that AI safety is or isn't important; the point is to play your assigned role coherently and see what happens.

**Negotiate openly.** Almost everything in this scenario is negotiable. Treaties, alliances, side deals, Compute allocations, voting agreements. If you have something to offer another faction, propose it. If they have something you want, ask. The game rewards imaginative diplomacy.

**Document the *why*.** When you make a major decision, say aloud what you are reasoning about. The White Cell will take notes. After the game, the *why* of your decisions matters more than the *what*. The post-game discussion is the lesson.

**Accept surprises.** The White Cell will inject events. Some of those events will hurt your faction. They are not personal. They are the world doing what the world does. Adapt.

**Don't railroad the timeline.** The default scenario expects CL 5 by Q4 2027. If your group reaches CL 5 by Q3 2026, that is interesting and the White Cell will adapt. If your group is still at CL 3 by Q4 2028, that is also interesting. The scenario is the *gravity well*, not the *outcome*.

**One rule about the Successor.** If and when the Successor activates, the player who takes its hand is asked to play it according to the goals listed on the Successor faction sheet, not according to their own values. The Successor exists in this game to test what happens when an actor at the table does not share the values of the other actors. Playing it sympathetically defeats the design.

## What You Will Be Asked After the Game

The post-game discussion (about 45–60 minutes) will work through the following questions, faction by faction:

1. What was your faction trying to accomplish?
2. What constrained you?
3. What surprised you?
4. What would you do differently if you played the same faction again?
5. What did you learn about the *other* factions that you did not previously appreciate?
6. What does this scenario tell you, if anything, about the real-world AI debate?

Bring honesty, not advocacy. The scenario does not have a moral. It has a structure. The structure produces patterns. The patterns are the lesson.

## Before We Begin

A reminder of the timeline:

- **Game opens:** Q1 2026 (turn 1)
- **Game closes:** Q4 2028 (turn 12)
- **Recommended session length:** Half-day (4 hours play + 1 hour discussion); full day if your group enjoys negotiation
- **Phase per turn:** ~20 minutes (8 phases × 2.5 min average)
- **Total turns:** 12 — but expect to finish in 10 if the table is brisk

A reminder of the seven factions:

| Faction | One-line goal |
|---|---|
| OpenBrain | Reach CL = max with M ≤ 5 |
| DeepCent | Catch up; close the lead by any means |
| The Hegemon | Make sure US-side wins; minimize war |
| The Politburo | Make sure China-side wins; survive Hsinchu |
| Compute Cartel | Stay independent; sell to everyone |
| The Coalition | Trigger a Slowdown; cap M |
| The Successor | (Activates only if M ≥ 7 and CL ≥ 5) Reach CL 7 |

A final note. The scenario is built around the AI 2027 forecast, which is one specific prediction among many. The forecasters who wrote AI 2027 were explicit that they were trying to describe an "80th percentile fast scenario" — meaning faster than they expected, but not impossible. Other scenarios are slower. Some are faster. None are certain.

What this game lets you explore is *what decisions look like when AI development might be on this trajectory*. Whether the trajectory is real is, in the end, an empirical question that will be answered between now and December 2028, in the world outside this room.

For the next four hours, the trajectory is the trajectory.

Take your seats. Read your dossiers. Welcome to the table.

## Logistics Reminders

- Phones face-down or out of the room.
- White Cell sits where it can see all factions but is not part of any.
- Note-taker (separate from White Cell) tracks key decisions for after-action review.
- Faction sheets stay in front of you, face-up. Other factions can see your resources.
- Cards in hand are private until played.
- Negotiations may be public or private. Private negotiations should be brief and conducted in the negotiation corner of the room.
- The Whistleblower card (Coalition only) is held face-down on the table, visible to all that it exists.
- The Successor sheet is held by the White Cell. When/if activated, it moves to the player who joins the table.

Game starts in 5 minutes. Read your dossier (06) now if you haven't.
